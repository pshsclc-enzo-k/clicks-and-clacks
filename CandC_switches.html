Types of Mechanical Keyswitches

All mechanical keyboards have a switch that is used to register a keypress. This is the main difference between mechanical keyboards and membrane keyboards, and the main reason for the high price point of some custom keyboard builds.

Linear Switches

Linear switches are a type of switch that feels smooth on the keypress. They don't have a tactile event when pressed, and the point of actuation is not discernable through pressing. This type of switch is best used for gaming, as it is smooth when pressed. When you play games on your pc, entering a ton of things at once (commonly referred to as "buttonmashing") is easy and feels consistent. The noise the switch makes is usually fairly quiet, but not totally silent (unless specifically stated otherwise).

Tactile Switches

Tactile switches are a type of switch that has a noticeable bump when pressed. Unlike a linear switch, the switch isn't smooth on press, and the actuation point is discernable by keypress alone. This type of switch is best for typing, where making mistakes is less likely due to the aforementioned bump. Tactile switches are generally louder than linear switches, but some tactile switches are dampened to become silent.

Clicky Switches

Clicky switches are a type of switch that not only has a noticeable bump when pressed, but also an audible click noise. The actuation point is discernable through both the bump and the click noise. This type of switch is for people who remember the old days of computer terminals. The audible click noise that these switches make is an homage to the keyboards of old, such as the legendary IBM Model M. However, in today's standards, most people usually avoid clicky switches, as the noise can be distracting and irritating, especially in the context of offices.
